Maiia Pohrebniak

RunCTRL

UI/UX Design, Branding

Project Brief:

My task was to develop an app of a world known running program. It would help people who are not used to running or don’t know anything about it, to run 5k in just 9 weeks. The app should be developed for native IOS users in a futuristic style. The first release of the app would be an MVP and other features would be added later in some time.

Phase 1: Research

In order to better understand the needs and priorities of potential RunCTRL users, I conducted a competitive analysis of similar applications to identify common features. It appears that there are only a few apps with this specific program on the market. And seeing, that there is not enough data, I also decided to research other apps for running and personalised training so that I could include some useful features into RunCTRL app.

The research generated critical insights of what users love using in the apps that already exist and helped me to highlight the desirable features that they wanted to see in such kind of applications.

Based on the feature research that other applications have, I wanted to learn more about user needs, their demographic, their reasons to start running and how much time they are ready to dedicate to this. The survey covered the information about the users in general and what they expect to see in such kind of app. Here are the key highlights:

Using my findings from the research, survey and a few user interviews with potential users, I summed up all the information and put it into a Persona.

Phase 2: Information architecture: sitemap and user flow

Informed by both the primary and secondary research findings, I drafted an application map to organize content and features.

I also designed a user flow to represent some common paths that a user might take in interacting with the product, and to think through the possible outcomes of a given flow. Based on common flow patterns identified in the competitive analysis, I outlined flows for getting started with the run just when you land, seeing your progress, viewing and having easy access to the workout analysis.

Phase 3: Wireframing + Prototyping

Through diagramming user flows, I identified key pages that I would need to design, and sketched them with pen and paper. Then I quickly transitioned to designing a low-fidelity prototype in Adobe Xd.

Phase 4: Usability testing + Iteration + User Interface

After wireframing and prototyping the first pages, I showed the prototype to the developers, letting them to walk through the project personally and asking them questions on each page. It helped me with two things: 1. Identify any problems from the developers side. Even though they were aware of all the steps I was taking and I was showing them the progress, I wanted their confirmation of all the features, so that they would know the way of how to develop. 2. I got the potential user’s feedback and made some changes accordingly.

I also conducted usability testing with people who might use such services in the future in order to identify potential errors and issues when engaging with the prototype. I tested the app in order to better understand the level of clarity of the content and structure, asking users to describe what they expect to see on the next page, how would they change a certain icon etc. I also gave them different tasks to complete in order to see how smooth the process was for them. Finally, I summerized the results and made the changes in my prototype accordingly.

The key findings from Usability Testing

I wish there were controls to skip for example the walking warm up or go back and do it again.
The Rewind Button feels like disabled.
Not clear enough what is on or off on Running settings page, e.g. Total Distance seems like ON.
Settings icon on Running page and in the bottom navigation seems like the page with the same content.

After a few iterations and edits, I managed to come to the final version of the prototype and work on the User Interface.

One of the challenges was to create a RunCTRL logo, the other one - to make the app in a futuristic style, making the user want to tap on the next page, making them curious and engaged.

The process of exploration of the colours, fonts and style started from articles on Medium and ended on such platforms as dribbble seeking the visual inspiration.
